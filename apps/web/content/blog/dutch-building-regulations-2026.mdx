The Dutch construction industry is in the middle of a regulatory shift. The *Omgevingswet* (Environment and Planning Act), which took effect in January 2024, consolidated dozens of existing laws into a single framework. For contractors, two pieces matter most: the **BBL** and the **Wkb**.

## BBL — Besluit bouwwerken leefomgeving

The BBL replaces the old *Bouwbesluit 2012* and sets the technical requirements for buildings: structural safety, fire resistance, energy performance, acoustics, ventilation, and more.

Key differences from Bouwbesluit:

- **Performance-based** — more room for alternative solutions, as long as you can demonstrate they meet the performance requirement
- **Integrated with Omgevingswet** — permit applications go through the DSO (Digitaal Stelsel Omgevingswet) instead of separate municipal systems
- **Environmental norms** — stricter requirements for sustainability (MPG scores, BENG norms for energy)

## Wkb — quality assurance

The Wkb shifts quality oversight from municipalities to independent kwaliteitsborgers for consequence class 1 buildings. For contractors, this means:

- The **contractor** is primarily responsible for quality and liable for defects after handover
- A **kwaliteitsborger** monitors compliance during construction and produces findings
- The **contractor** must resolve findings with evidence before the building can be handed over
- The **municipality** receives a dossier bevoegd gezag at gereedmelding — a complete evidence package proving compliance

For a deeper dive on Wkb specifically, see our post on [WKB compliance explained](/blog/wkb-compliance-explained).

## The contractor's documentation burden

Both BBL and Wkb assume a level of documentation that is hard to maintain manually. A contractor building a block of 20 apartments needs to:

- Track three filing deadlines per project (bouwmelding, informatieplicht, gereedmelding)
- Collect and verify certificates from dozens of suppliers
- Respond to inspection findings with resolution evidence
- Compile everything into a dossier bevoegd gezag at completion
- Retain all evidence for 10 years (the contractor's liability period)

Across multiple concurrent projects, this becomes unmanageable with email and shared drives.

## What's coming next

The Netherlands is moving toward mandatory BIM for public projects. The *Rijksvastgoedbedrijf* already requires IFC deliverables, and more municipalities are following.

For contractors, this means:

- Digital compliance documentation becomes the baseline expectation
- Tools that bridge the gap between the kwaliteitsborger's inspections and the contractor's dossier will be essential
- The 10-year liability period makes tamper-evident audit trails non-optional
